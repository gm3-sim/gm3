Abstract

Modeling micro-mobility vehicles (MMVs) is increasingly important for autonomous driving validation and urban traffic simulation, yet most practical tools still rely on variants of the Kinematic Bicycle Model (KBM) or mode-specific approximations. These approaches are efficient, but they underrepresent tire slip, load transfer, rider/vehicle lean, and non-standard wheel layouts. GM3 addresses this gap with a unified, tire-level physical formulation grounded in the brush tire model.

GM3 supports arbitrary wheel configurations, including single-track, double-track, and multi-wheel platforms. The model captures nonlinear tire behavior while staying computationally practical for interactive simulation. We also provide a model-agnostic evaluation framework that separates platform geometry from dynamics, enabling side-by-side comparison across models under consistent controls and integration settings.

Across Stanford Drone Dataset trajectories and aggressive maneuver tests, GM3 improves step-by-step trajectory fidelity (ADE) relative to KBM while maintaining comparable global path similarity (DFD). These results indicate that tire-level dynamics provide meaningful gains for realistic MMV modeling, especially in higher-curvature or dynamically demanding scenarios.

Introduction

Micro-mobility use continues to grow in urban spaces, and so does the need to model interactions between MMVs and autonomous systems. Existing motion models are often vehicle-centric and tuned for low-speed, low-curvature conditions. This leads to unrealistic behavior when MMVs execute hard turns, evasive maneuvers, or rapid changes in acceleration.

KBM remains a common default due to simplicity, but it assumes geometric rolling constraints that omit key effects present in real MMVs. GM3 is designed to preserve practical simulation speed while introducing physically grounded mechanics: tire-force saturation, slip coupling, longitudinal/lateral load transfer, and lean-induced moments.

The objective is a single general model that can represent bicycles, scooters, skateboards, carts, and novel wheel layouts using a unified formulation rather than separate per-vehicle dynamics pipelines.

Methodology Overview

GM3 update loop: load transfer and lean conversion, control assignment per tire, tire-force computation, and integration into vehicle state.

Vehicle Integration

Each tire receives transformed body velocities in its local frame, computes brush-model forces, and maps these forces back to the body frame. Total forces and yaw moment are aggregated across all tires, including self-aligning moments.

Load Transfer

Normal load is redistributed longitudinally and laterally based on acceleration, center-of-gravity height, wheelbase, and track width. This ensures each tire-force computation uses realistic per-wheel normal forces.

Rider Lean Dynamics

For lean-capable platforms, rider center-of-gravity shifts generate roll moments coupled with centripetal effects. These terms are integrated into the global vehicle force/moment update.

Skateboard Truck Geometry

GM3 supports lean-to-steer skateboards through opposite front/rear truck steering angles induced by kingpin geometry. This reproduces turn behavior not captured by standard steering assumptions.

Generalization Across Platform Layouts

GM3 supports common, uncommon, and novel wheel configurations under a single dynamics interface.

Interactive Evaluation Framework

Model-agnostic runtime loop with fixed-step RK4 integration, interactive/scripted control input, visual trajectory tracing, and CSV export.

The framework separates dynamics from rendering and control interfaces. This allows the same platform geometry to be tested with GM3, KBM, and future models under equivalent integration and input constraints. Supported control modes include direct steering, lean-to-steer mappings, and differential-drive behavior.

Experiments and Results

Evaluation uses trajectories from the Stanford Drone Dataset deathCircle scene and additional high-curvature maneuver traces. We compare GM3 and KBM with Average Displacement Error (ADE) and Discrete Frechet Distance (DFD).

GM3 consistently improves ADE across biker, skater, and cart classes, indicating stronger local trajectory fidelity. DFD outcomes are comparable overall, with mode-specific gains for skater and cart trajectories and minor tradeoffs for biker tracks where lean-induced lateral offsets are more pronounced.

On aggressive maneuvers including S-curves, U-turns, and turn-intensive patterns, GM3 better reproduces high-curvature trajectory structure by modeling slip and load transfer directly at the tire level.

Conclusion

GM3 introduces a general and physically grounded MMV model that unifies tire-level dynamics across varied platform layouts while preserving practical simulation performance. Compared with KBM baselines, GM3 delivers more realistic local trajectory behavior and competitive path-level reconstruction.

Future directions include differentiable formulations for learning and control, larger instrumented datasets for parameter identification, and tighter integration into multi-agent autonomous-system simulation pipelines.
